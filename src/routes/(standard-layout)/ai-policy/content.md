I have numerous reservations on the usage of AI in a money-first society, _and_ there are cases where I find its use acceptable. This document is meant to reflect where I personally do (and _don't_) use AI. ## Some background

### Non-LLM and Non-Generative AI

"AI" (Artificial Intelligence) and "LLM" (Large Language Model) are important to distinguish. AI has existed since before the Internet; an arcade machine playing Pong is an example of an artificially intelligent agent, albeit a simple one. LLMs are relatively newer (though still older than 2020), and use language as a method for decision-making, rather than pure logic.

Another axis worth distinguishing is "generative" and "non-generative" AI. The general goal of AI is to make things things easier for people (keep cars in lane, automate video game monsters, detect tumors). Generative AI's goal is to just make things. Especially things that people can make.

For the rest of this page, **AI** will refer to "Generative LLM-based AI", which is the kind of AI people actually find contentious.


> [!NOTE]
> LLMs are Neural Networks, but Neural Networks are not LLMs. There are many very useful neural networks that are not LLMs, such as AlphaFold in the medical field.

### Points For and Against AI

Generative LLM-based AI is contentious for these reasons:

* It was trained by using (perhaps legally stealing) the output of humans without consent.
* It displaces (or threatens to displace) jobs without providing alternative recourse.
* It crowds out human-made content with a sea of low-effort output (dilutes creativity).
* It enables scamming and cheating at unprecedented scales.
* It erodes the truth with intentional and unintentional misinformation, then uses that to train itself.
* It requires a significant amount of energy to train and run.
* It generates revenue for companies that will not turn that around as value for humans.
* It encourages social isolation and mental psychosis (some deaths can be attributed to AI specifically).
* It is potentially existentially dangerous (for example, by trivializing the creation of pathogens).

Generative LLM-based AI has the following benefits with responsible use:

* It finds associations in scientific literature previously missed, because no one can read at scale.
* It accelerates the development of software (infamously too much so at times).
* It aids with research by finding, reading, synthesizing, and citing sources.
* It focuses self-directed learning

The key word there is "responsible" use, because unlike most tools, it is _extremely easy_ to use AI irresponsibly. Blindly accepting code output, not reading sources it cites for veracity, etc. are all examples of failures to engage critical thinking.

> [!CAUTION]
> A common non-point I hear is that AI democratizes artistic expression, but I do not believe this to be a valid point. "Craft" and "art" are, at least in my opinion, inseperable; you cannot make a "drawing" unless you learn how to actually draw. However, AI _can_ be a useful tool to _learn_ how to draw, by tightening a feedback loop before you ask real humans for feedback.

## Guiding Principles

* Do not let AI automate your brain. Use your intelligence, protect your creativity.
* Do not let AI do what you do for you. Instead, use AI to help you do what you do faster. 

### AI in Code

I use AI as a regular pair programmer. Practically, this means:

* I ask AI for small snippets of code. Most of the time this is a single function, occasionally I ask for some boilerplate.
* I ask AI how to use certain libraries or tools, especially when said libraries have dense documentation.
* AI provides library recommendations to serve as a starting point for research.
* I get AI's input on architectural decisions I make, sort of like an editor.
* Where I use AI for code, I try to commit the prompt as use as well, treating prompt-as-code.

I do not "vibe code", where vibe code means letting AI code most of the application autonomously. I [conducted a vibe code experiment](/posts/vibe-coding-experiment/) to teach myself its limitations, and in my opinion, a human understanding of a codebase is still important. Perhaps in the future it won't be, but that future is not today. These are the biggest limitations I find:

* AI struggles to verify its own code.
* AI does not write accessible web code. I constantly have to correct it.
* AI makes enough security mistakes that human audit is necessary.
* AI does not inherently know your cross functional requirements.
* AI is trained on most _common_ practices, not _best_ practices.



### AI in Writing

I firmly believe in human writing, and I do not let AI write anything that I post. I do use AI for organizing thoughts, conducting preliminary research, and as a first-pass editor. This helps me with structure and tightening (goodness knows I'm ranty if left to my own devices).

* Diversity makes the world colorful, and our personal voice contributes to that diversity. I'd rather sound mediocre and _me_ than good but robotic.
* Large Language Models excel at exactly one thing, and that's language. If it has any explicit purpose at all, using it to _improve_ our writing is it (note: **not** replace our writing).



### AI in Art

I do not use AI to create art, and in general people should avoid using AI to create art. I do believe in the potential for AI as a kind of "brush" to automate things artists broadly find tedious, but I have yet to see convincing examples.

There's something special about the human voice in art that AI fails to capture. For example, I've tried asking AI to generate images that best embody "loss", and you get generic images of a sad person, sometimes at a gravesite. I looked up human art of the same thing, and I got an image of someone at a grave but this time wearing a mask. AI makes pictures, humans tell stories.
